Guess what I was today!

Fired.

It was only a voluntary job, but it was still the best damn job I ever had.

My job was to Web master for South Square Centre. I put a lot of time and effort into it. See screen dump from today (my lovely Trinity style!).

I was fired because the trainee curator of the gallery apparently has to “design and maintain a Web site” as part of her training. I personally think it’s a bad idea to force people who may know nothing about computers to “design and maintain a Web site”, especially when there may be better people for the job. If a curator trained at the Tate (very popular gallery in London), for example, they probably wouldn’t be allowed to create and maintain the Tate’s Web site. Why would whatever body is examining Bob expect her to be able to do things in more than one field well? It’s like expecting computer geeks to have a thorough understanding of sociology. Curators should curate and Web designers should design Web sites.

I have no ill will towards Bob or anybody else at South Square. I’m just a little pissed-off as I’m sure is understandable.

I spent so much effort on that Web site. I wrote a CMS, I wrote a Makefile to simplify backups (MySQL and source dumps), compiling (using the Python code to generate a static copy of the data in the MySQL database), uploading and cleaning (deleting everything that can be re-generated). I even did a fucking usability test! Sure the test wasn’t very good, but I got vital information from it (the participant thought the “gallery” anchor would link to a gallery of art rather than information about the gallery).

For completeness: Here’s the final dump of the source and MySQL table. Anything written by me (i.e. the code) is GPL, anything written by others (i.e. some of the site content) is copyright them.

Oh, and I had the Foundations of HCI exam (my only exam this exam period) today. It didn’t go too well. Terribly, in fact—as bad as my psychology exams went last year and the year before that, I’d say. I understand and love HCI but I just couldn’t answer the questions, much like psychology. I know and can apply the knowledge but it doesn’t come through in my exams.

All-in-all, not a good day.

“What I want to do when I grow up”

Inspired by Buncey’s most recent entry, I shall now speak at great length (yeah, right) about what I wanted to do when I grew up (I’m unsure as to whether it should be ‘grew’ or ‘grow’).

My first memory of “what I want to do when I grow up” was that I wanted to be an aerodynamic engineer. Probably because at that time I had a preoccupation with paper aeroplanes. Coming to think of it, it was almost like programming. There was a car advert where this man threw a paper aeroplane and it came back to him; he’d designed it such that it would fly in a circle and return to him. The concept of aerodynamics fascinated me.

I couldn’t help mentioning it: Theme Park. Great game. But I never wanted to be a theme park manager or lay rubber tubing (or tubbing as I referred to it). That’s just crazy.

Next I wanted to be a game developer. Probably around the time I was very into Oddworld (i.e. before Munch’s Oddysee and before Oddworld Forums imploded). I expect I realised game development involved effort (back then I didn’t know about ‘crunches’ and EA Games). Then I gave up on that idea. I think I was doing very simple things in C and C++ back then.

Then several years past where I had no idea what I wanted to do “when I grew up”. I wouldn’t have minded but those few years included my A-Levels where knowing what I wanted to do could have been useful. In fact when I applied to UCAS for university last December (late December—possibly even early January—when the deadline was 17 January (2004-01-17)) I applied to several Teaching courses and “Interactive Multimedia” at Lincoln University (on the Hull campus). I later regretted this decision and applied through Clearing to the University of Bradford to do Computer Science. I didn’t quite have the grades but they obviously had a few spare places, which was good because the first year is mostly below me so the foundation year (which I had the UCAS points for) would have been pure hell.

And do I know what I want to do “when I grow up” now? I think a career in Web design would be good. Coding. Possibly even some usability work.

At the moment I don’t know whether I want to work for a company or work freelance or even start my own small business. The small business idea could be fun. There’d be a server running CVS so everybody could contribute to one project without excessive emails of “here’s the file…” and there’d be a Wiki. But looking past the fun part, it would be difficult to organise and possibly hard to get work. I could always do that as a side job, possibly with a friend or two if it’s a big project.
